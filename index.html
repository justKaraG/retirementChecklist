The 4 Decisions They Need to Make

Decision 1 ‚Äî The Big One: Original Medicare or Medicare Advantage?
Original Medicare (Parts A + B)
  • ‚úÖ See any doctor nationwide that accepts Medicare
  • ‚úÖ No referrals needed for specialists
  • ‚úÖ Can add Medigap to cap out-of-pocket costs
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è No dental, vision, or hearing included
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Need to buy Part D separately
Medicare Advantage (Part C)
  • ‚úÖ Often includes dental, vision, hearing
  • ‚úÖ Drug coverage (Part D) usually bundled in
  • ‚úÖ Can have lower premiums
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Must use plan's network of doctors
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Cannot add Medigap if you change your mind later
Steve and Jody choose independently ‚Äî they don't have to pick the same option.
Decision 2 ‚Äî Part D Drug Plan (required if on Original Medicare)

Each person picks a Part D plan based on their specific prescriptions. Plans vary by which drugs they cover and at what cost. Compare using your actual medication list at medicare.gov/plan-compare.

‚ö†Ô∏è Even if you take no prescriptions right now ‚Äî enroll anyway. The late penalty is permanent (1% per month delayed).

If choosing Medicare Advantage, Part D is usually included ‚Äî no separate plan needed.

Decision 3 ‚Äî Medigap Supplement? (Original Medicare path only)

Original Medicare covers ~80% of costs ‚Äî Medigap pays most or all of the remaining 20%, plus deductibles. Monthly premium varies by plan type (A through N) and insurer.

Plan G (most popular)
Covers almost everything except the Part B deductible (~$257/yr in 2026). Most comprehensive option available to new enrollees.
Plan N (lower premium)
Lower monthly cost but you pay small copays at the doctor ($20) and ER ($50). Good if you're healthy and rarely go.

‚ö†Ô∏è Must apply within 6 months of enrolling in Part B ‚Äî after that, insurers can deny based on health history.

Decision 4 ‚Äî When to Take Part B (if still working or covered by employer)

If Steve is retiring now with no employer coverage continuing, take Part B immediately during the Initial Enrollment Period. If he has creditable employer coverage continuing (e.g. retiree plan), he may be able to delay ‚Äî but must get that confirmed in writing from HR.

Jody follows the same logic ‚Äî if she's covered under Steve's employer plan, she may delay too, but both need the creditable coverage letter.

‚ö†Ô∏è When in doubt, enroll now. The late penalty is 10% per year delayed ‚Äî for life.

Important: Jody's situation is different from Steve's

As a stay-at-home mom, Jody qualifies for Medicare and Social Security through Steve's work record ‚Äî not her own. She cannot apply online for Medicare (must call or visit SSA in person). Her Medicare and Social Security benefits also depend on Steve acting first in several cases.

Top Things NOT to Miss

Jody cannot enroll in Medicare online

Because she has no personal work record, she must call SSA at 1-800-772-1213 or visit an office in person to enroll. Online enrollment won't work for her situation.

Jody's spousal SS benefits require Steve to file first

Jody cannot claim spousal Social Security benefits until Steve has filed for his own benefits. Plan the timing together.

Medigap open enrollment is a one-time 6-month window

Both Steve and Jody each have a 6-month window starting when they turn 65 AND have Part B. After it closes, insurers can deny coverage or charge more based on health conditions.

Part D ‚Äî enroll even if you take no prescriptions

The 1%/month late penalty is permanent. Both need a Part D plan (or Medicare Advantage with drug coverage) to avoid it.

Jody's Medicare enrolls through Steve's work record

Jody qualifies for premium-free Part A based on Steve's 40+ quarters. She must call SSA at 1-800-772-1213 or visit an office ‚Äî she cannot use the online application. Her enrollment also requires that Steve is at least 62.

Late Enrollment Penalties ‚Äî Permanent Monthly Costs

Part B Penalty

+10% / year late

2-year delay = 20% added forever. On 2026's $202.90/mo: that's $40.58/month more for life. Each person pays their own penalty.

Part D Penalty

+1% / month late

14 months late = 14% permanently added to Part D premium. Based on $38.99/mo base in 2026. Both must enroll.

Part A Penalty

+10% premium

For those who pay a Part A premium. Applies for twice the years you were late. Steve and Jody likely qualify for free Part A, so this may not apply.

Exception ‚Äî Creditable Employer Coverage:

If either is covered by a current employer's health plan that is "creditable," you can delay Medicare without penalties. You get an 8-month Special Enrollment Period after that coverage ends. Get written confirmation from HR.

Medigap ‚Äî One-Time Window Per Person

When: 6 months starting the first day of the month you are 65+ AND enrolled in Part B.

Guaranteed rights during this window: Insurers must accept you at standard rates regardless of health conditions or pre-existing conditions.

After window closes: Insurers can deny your application or charge significantly higher premiums based on health status. There is no federal guarantee they must sell to you.

Important for Jody:

Jody's 6-month Medigap window is tied to HER enrollment date in Part B, not Steve's. If they enroll at different times, their windows are different. Don't miss it.
